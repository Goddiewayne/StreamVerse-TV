[27 June 2026, Randburg — Johannesburg, DStv City building, 02:47 AM]

The Irdeto smart card sat in the foam cradle like a dead thing, its golden contact pads winking under the desk lamp.

Kael turned it over with tweezers. *Ninety-two cents of plastic and epoxy, and it holds fifteen years of conditional access engineering.* He'd lifted it from a Premium decoder in Fourways — paid the installer five hundred rand to "forget" it at the bottom of his toolbox. The man thought it was a firmware update dongle. *Let him think that.*

The rental server room hummed behind him. Two U of rack space, a MikroTik router, and a Dell R630 he'd bought off Carbonite with a dodgy iLO password still active. His heartbeat in the quiet was louder than the fans.

He plugged the card into the Omnikey reader. A green LED blinked once. Then twice. Then steady.

"Talk to me, you little bitch."

He typed:

```
opensc-tool -a
```

The card answered: `3B 9F 21 0E 49 52 44 45 54 4F 20 32 2E 33 01`. Irdeto 2.3. The same string he'd seen in a hundred datasheets, but on-screen, live, from an actual card — that was different. That was a heartbeat.

"The cock's in your mouth, darling." *Now show me your teeth.*

[Same date, DStv City NOC, 2 AM (forty-seven minutes earlier)]

Petrus Mokoena scratched his chin and watched the seventeen monitors in the Network Operations Centre. Most were green. The occasional amber graph blipped on a transponder load. He'd been doing this since 2010 — back when DStv was still running MPEG-2 on the old PAS-8 bird — and nothing exciting ever happened after midnight.

*I'll be dead in this chair. Thirty years, same chair, same coffee, same nothing.*

His phone buzzed. A ping from the smart card pairing system: a decoder in Fourways had reported a card removal event at 01:58, then reinsertion at 02:01. Standard. Probably the owner cleaning the contacts. Petrus yawned and swiped the notification away.

[Same date, rental server room, 03:12 AM]

Kael had the Irdeto shell open. On his laptop, a custom Python script was spraying APDU commands at the card — SELECT FILE, GET DATA, READ BINARY — mapping the file system like a blind man reading a room with his tongue.

"Come on. Where's your fucking ECM key?"

Irdeto 2 stored its entitlements in DF E0, DF E1, the inner slots. Every provider set them up different. MultiChoice was smart — they'd buried the control word generator behind an RSA-authenticated session. You couldn't just READ BINARY on the key file. You had to challenge the card, prove you were a legitimate decoder, and wait for it to spit out the decrypted CW.

He built the challenge script. Ninety lines that hammered the card with the exact same handshake his Xtraview decoder used.

```
apdu = [
    0xA0, 0xA4, 0x00, 0x00, 0x02, 0xE0, 0x00,  // SELECT DF E0
    0xA0, 0xA8, 0x00, 0x00, 0x08,                // GET CHALLENGE
    0xA0, 0xAA, 0x00, 0x00, ...                  // AUTHENTICATE
]
```

He ran it.

The card paused. A full three seconds — which in smart card time was geological. Then came the response: `90 00`. Success. Kael let out a breath.

"Fuck me. We're in."

The script dumped the control word. A hex string: `4F B3 8C 1A D5 76 E2 9F`. Sixteen bytes of pure decryption authority. He could feed this to ffmpeg and decrypt any Irdeto-encrypted transport stream on that transponder.

He cracked a beer. Black Label. Warm. Perfect.

[03:24 AM, rental server room]

Kael had the CW piped into a test stream. On his laptop, VLC opened a window: SuperSport Premier League's test card — the one they played between 2 AM and 4 AM — a looping promo for the FIFA World Cup. In crystal-clear, decrypted, 1080p glory.

He laughed. *You beautiful, broken, beautiful system.*

Then the iLO interface on his Dell flickered. A remote login. From an IP range that started with 196.42 — the MultiChoice corporate block.

"Fuck."

He yanked the smart card reader's USB cable. Grabbed the laptop. Left the server running — nothing on it was his real identity — and walked out the fire exit into the parking lot. His heart jackhammering, the card reader bouncing against his thigh inside the backpack.

He didn't run. Running got you noticed. He walked to the rented Golf, started it, and pulled out onto Bram Fischer Drive, headlights off until he hit the main road.

Ninety-two cents of plastic and epoxy, still in his bag, still spitting Irdeto keys like a broken confession.

The sky was purple over Johannesburg. Morning shift was coming.

*Eight hours of access. Plenty of time to burn.*
